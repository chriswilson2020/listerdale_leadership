The Other Half of the Conversation

The Science and Art of Receiving Feedback Well

Most leadership training focuses on giving feedback. But the ability to receive feedback, especially when it's hard to hear, is what separates leaders who grow from those who plateau. As Douglas Stone and Sheila Heen argue: the receiver is the one who controls whether feedback leads to learning.

Why Receiving Feedback Is Harder Than Giving It

We spend enormous energy training people to give feedback. We teach them frameworks, scripts, and timing. And yet feedback cultures remain broken in most organisations. Stone and Heen's insight is that the bottleneck isn't the giver, it's the receiver.

When a senior leader can receive feedback well, the entire organisation's feedback culture shifts. When they can't, no amount of training for givers will fix it. People watch how leaders respond to hard truths, and they calibrate their honesty accordingly.

The challenge is biological: feedback, especially critical feedback, triggers a threat response. Your heart rate increases, your thinking narrows, and your instinct is to defend, deflect, or dismiss. Learning to override that instinct is a skill, not a personality trait.

The Three Feedback Triggers

Stone and Heen identified three triggers that block our ability to hear feedback. Each is provoked by something different, and each requires a different strategy to manage.

T

Truth Trigger

The feedback itself seems wrong, unfair, or unhelpful

"That's not what happened"

R

Relationship Trigger

The person giving it is the problem, not the feedback

"Who are you to tell me this?"

I

Identity Trigger

The feedback threatens your sense of who you are

"I'm not that kind of person"

How to Manage Each Trigger

  • Truth triggers: Separate the "what" from the "who." Even if the feedback is poorly delivered, ask yourself: is there a kernel of truth here? Get curious about the data behind their observation.
  • Relationship triggers: Separate the feedback from the relationship. The message and the messenger are two different things. You can address relationship issues separately, but first, hear the feedback on its own terms.
  • Identity triggers: Recognise that feedback about one behaviour is not a verdict on your entire character. Move from "I am" to "I did." You are not your last mistake.

The 5 A's: A Framework for Receiving Feedback

Michelle Tillis Lederman's 5 A's framework provides a practical step-by-step process for handling feedback gracefully, even when your instinct is to push back.

1

Absorb

Listen with a level head. No defending, no explaining. Just take it in.

2

Ask

Probe deeper. "Can you give me an example?" "What would improvement look like?"

3

Acknowledge

Summarise what you heard. Agree with at least one specific point.

4

Appreciate

Thank them. Giving honest feedback is hard too. Invite them to keep doing it.

5

Apply

Act on it. Follow up. Show them their feedback made a difference.

The Feedback Staircase

When we receive critical feedback, most of us move through a predictable emotional sequence. Knowing the staircase helps you recognise where you are, and climb faster.

Deny "Not true"
Defend "But I..."
Explain "Because..."
Understand "I see it"
Change "I'll act"

The goal isn't to skip steps, it's to move through them faster. Most people get stuck at Defend or Explain.

Three Types of Feedback: Know What You're Getting

Not all feedback is the same. Mismatches between what you need and what you're getting cause frustration on both sides.

Appreciation

"I see you. I value you."

Meets the need for recognition. Without it, coaching and evaluation feel hollow.

Coaching

"Here's how to improve."

Aimed at growth. Most useful when you're ready for it. Least useful when you needed appreciation first.

Evaluation

"Here's where you stand."

Tells you how you measure up. Necessary but threatening. Once you hear evaluation, you stop hearing everything else.

Pro tip: If you're getting coaching but you needed appreciation, say so: "I want to hear your suggestions, but first, can you tell me what's working?" Naming the mismatch prevents resentment.

Practical Techniques

Before the Conversation

  • Ask for it proactively: When you ask, you set the terms, timing, mindset, even topic. You arrive prepared instead of ambushed.
  • Prepare your mind: Tell yourself: "This is going to be uncomfortable, and that's the point. Feedback is data, not a verdict."
  • Pick your question: "What's one thing I could do differently?" is less overwhelming than "How am I doing?"

During the Conversation

  • Don't get mad, get curious: Fred Kofman's phrase from Conscious Business. Repeat it internally. Curiosity turns threat into problem-solving.
  • Label and reappraise: Name the emotion ("I'm feeling defensive right now") and reframe the situation ("This person is trying to help me").
  • Listen for the 2%: Even if 98% of the feedback feels wrong, find the 2% that's true. That's where the growth is.
  • Take notes: It keeps you focused, signals respect, and gives you something to review when the emotions have settled.

After the Conversation

  • Sleep on it: Your first reaction is rarely your best reaction. Give yourself 24 hours before deciding what to do with the feedback.
  • Sort and filter: Not all feedback deserves action. Some is wrong. Some is right but not right now. Some is exactly what you needed to hear.
  • Close the loop: Go back to the person. Tell them what you did with their feedback. This is the single most powerful thing you can do to encourage future honesty.

Scripts for Receiving Feedback

When You Disagree

  • "I hear you. I see it differently, but I want to understand your perspective better. Can you walk me through what you saw?"
  • "That's interesting, I hadn't thought about it that way. Let me sit with it."

When It Stings

  • "Thank you for telling me. I need a moment to process this, but I want you to know I'm taking it seriously."
  • "This is hard to hear, but I'd rather hear it from you than not hear it at all."

When You Want More

  • "Can you give me a specific example so I can understand exactly what you mean?"
  • "What would it look like if I got this right?"
  • "Is there anything else you've been holding back? I'd rather hear it all now."

When Following Up

  • "You gave me feedback last month about X. I've been working on it, have you noticed any change?"
  • "I wanted to circle back on what you said. Here's what I did with it..."

Common Mistakes When Receiving Feedback

  • Defending immediately: The instinct to explain or justify shuts down the conversation before it starts
  • Counter-attacking: "Well, you do the same thing", this turns feedback into a fight
  • Dismissing the source: "They don't understand my role", relationship triggers hijacking truth
  • Agreeing to everything: Accepting all feedback uncritically is just as harmful as rejecting it all
  • Never following up: If you don't act on feedback or close the loop, people stop giving it
  • Conflating types: Hearing evaluation when coaching was intended, or needing appreciation but getting advice
  • Making it binary: Feedback isn't right or wrong, it's a data point. Treat it as information, not a verdict

Key Takeaway

Your ability to receive feedback determines how much truth you'll ever hear. If people learn that giving you feedback is painful, exhausting, or pointless, they'll stop. And then you're leading blind. The most powerful thing a leader can do is make it safe, and rewarding, for people to tell them the truth.
