What Stage of Growth Is Your Business In?

Identifying Your Current Phase to Lead Effectively

Business Growth Framework

Adapted from Greiner's Growth Model & Modern Startup Theory

Understanding your company's growth stage helps you prioritize the right activities, hire the right people, and develop the leadership skills needed for your current challenges.

Why Growth Stage Matters

The strategies that work brilliantly at one stage can become liabilities at the next. A founder who excels at the scrappy startup phase may struggle when the company needs formal processes. Understanding where you are helps you:

  • Focus on the right priorities for your current challenges
  • Hire people with skills appropriate to your stage
  • Avoid premature scaling or delayed professionalization
  • Recognize when it's time to evolve your leadership approach

Stage 1: Start-up

Typical size: 1-20 employees | Focus: Survival and Product-Market Fit

Characteristics

  • Founder(s) involved in everything; sales, product, hiring, customer support
  • Rapid iteration and pivoting based on market feedback
  • Informal communication, everyone knows what's happening
  • Culture forms organically around founding team's values
  • Cash conservation is critical; runway is always a concern
  • Hiring generalists who can wear multiple hats

Leadership Focus

  • Finding product-market fit before running out of money
  • Building the initial team and establishing culture
  • Making fast decisions with incomplete information
  • Staying close to customers to understand their needs

Common Mistakes

  • Scaling before achieving product-market fit
  • Over-hiring or building too much infrastructure too soon
  • Losing focus by chasing too many opportunities

Stage 2: Scale-up

Typical size: 20-200 employees | Focus: Building Systems and Teams

Characteristics

  • Founder transitions from doing to managing and enabling
  • Formal processes and structures become necessary
  • Specialized roles replace generalist positions
  • Middle management layer emerges
  • Culture must be intentionally maintained and communicated
  • Communication requires more structure and documentation

Leadership Focus

  • Building scalable systems and processes
  • Hiring specialists and experienced managers
  • Delegating effectively while maintaining quality
  • Preserving culture as the team grows rapidly
  • Developing the leadership team

Common Mistakes

  • Founder refusing to delegate or let go of control
  • Hiring too fast without proper onboarding
  • Neglecting culture as you focus on growth
  • Not investing in management training

Stage 3: Grown-up

Typical size: 200+ employees | Focus: Optimization and Innovation

Characteristics

  • Leadership focuses on vision, strategy, and culture
  • Multiple layers of management and specialized departments
  • Formal governance, compliance, and risk management
  • Innovation must be systematized to avoid stagnation
  • Communication spans multiple levels and requires intentional effort
  • Balancing efficiency with agility becomes a key challenge

Leadership Focus

  • Setting and communicating long-term vision
  • Building and developing the executive team
  • Maintaining entrepreneurial spirit within a larger organization
  • Managing board relationships and stakeholder expectations
  • Driving innovation while maintaining operational excellence

Common Mistakes

  • Becoming too bureaucratic and slow
  • Losing touch with customers and frontline employees
  • Failing to innovate and disrupt yourself
  • Neglecting culture as "someone else's job"

What Your Business Needs at Each Stage

Area Start-up Scale-up Grown-up
Hiring Generalists, hustlers Specialists, experienced managers Executives, strategic thinkers
Processes Minimal, flexible Building and documenting Optimizing and automating
Culture Organic, founder-driven Intentional, documented Reinforced, evolved
Communication Informal, constant Structured, regular Multi-channel, cascading
Founder Role Doer, decision-maker Manager, coach Visionary, culture keeper

Signs You're Ready to Move to the Next Stage

From Start-up to Scale-up:

  • You've achieved clear product-market fit with repeatable sales
  • The founder is becoming a bottleneck for decisions
  • You're turning away business because you can't deliver
  • Key processes exist only in people's heads
  • You have funding or revenue to support growth

From Scale-up to Grown-up:

  • You have a strong leadership team that can run operations
  • Core processes are documented and scalable
  • Culture is well-defined and consistently practiced
  • You're ready to focus on long-term strategy over daily operations
  • The organization can function when the founder is away

Key Takeaway

Knowing your stage helps you prioritize the right activities and develop the appropriate leadership skills. Don't try to run a 200-person company like a startup, and don't burden a startup with enterprise processes. Match your approach to your reality, and be ready to evolve as you grow.
